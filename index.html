How They Talk to Us

Cat Communication 101

😸

Vocalizations

Cats meow almost exclusively to communicate with humans — adult cats rarely meow at each other! Each cat develops a unique "vocabulary" for its owner.

🐈

Body Language

From the slow blink (a cat's kiss!) to an upright tail as a greeting, cats express a rich emotional world through posture, ears, and tail position.

😻

Purring

Cats purr not only when happy but also to self-soothe. The 25–50 Hz frequency of purring has even been linked to promoting bone healing in both cats and humans.

🐾

Scent Marking

Head-bunting and rubbing deposit scent glands on their favourite humans — it's a profound sign of trust and ownership (you belong to them now).

👁️

Eye Contact

A direct, relaxed gaze with slow blinks signals deep comfort. Research shows humans who slow-blink back strengthen the bond with their cats significantly.

🎶

Chirping & Trilling

The chirp (often directed at birds) and the trill (a greeting roll) are among the most delightful sounds in the feline repertoire — pure cat joy.

A History Together

From Grain Stores to Sofas

~10,000 BCE

Cats begin hanging around early farming settlements in the Fertile Crescent, attracted by rodents feeding on stored grain — a mutually beneficial arrangement that started it all.

~4,000 BCE

Ancient Egyptians worship the cat goddess Bastet. Cats become sacred animals, killing one punishable by death. They protect homes and temples from snakes and rodents.

Middle Ages

Ship's cats become indispensable crew members on voyages worldwide, keeping rats from destroying cargo and ropes. Dark times too — cats were associated with witchcraft and persecuted across Europe, ironically worsening the Black Plague by letting rat populations explode.

1500s

A cat takes up residence at 10 Downing Street, London, beginning a tradition of official pest control that continues to this day. The post is formally titled "Chief Mouser to the Cabinet Office" from 2011 onwards.

1940s–50s

Cat litter is invented in the mid-20th century, transforming cats from outdoor or barn animals into true indoor companions. This single innovation reshapes cat ownership across the Western world.

1985

Cats officially surpass dogs as the most popular pet in the United States for the first time — a milestone driven by their independence, apartment-friendliness, and the spread of indoor litter boxes.

Early 2000s

The internet discovers cats. Online communities like MetaFilter, 4chan, and early Japanese photo blogs popularise sharing cat pictures. Cats become the unofficial mascots of the World Wide Web — a phenomenon unlike any animal-human relationship in history.

2006–2007

LOLcats, Caturday, and the iconic "I Can Has Cheezburger?" website launch a new era of cat culture online. The first viral cat video — Puppy vs. Cat — goes global in 2006, proving cats and the internet are inseparable.

2011–2012

Nyan Cat, a pixelated pop-tart flying cat, racks up over 200 million YouTube views shortly after release. Grumpy Cat goes viral in 2012, eventually generating millions in merchandise and a Guinness World Record, becoming the first true celebrity cat of the social media age.

2015

The Museum of the Moving Image in New York City opens "How Cats Took Over the Internet" — the first major cultural institution to formally document cats' dominance of digital culture. Cat content is officially recognised as a force in modern media.

2019–2020

Global cat ownership surges during the COVID-19 pandemic years. With millions working from home, cat adoptions spike worldwide as people seek companionship. Studies confirm that living with cats measurably reduces anxiety and loneliness during lockdowns.

2020s

The "Cat Distribution System" meme goes viral — the idea that stray cats choose their humans, not the other way around. TikTok supercharges cat content to a new generation; cat-related videos routinely hit hundreds of millions of views. Estimated global cat population reaches up to 600 million.

2025

New genetic research rewrites cat domestication history, suggesting domestic cats originated specifically in North Africa and only reached Europe around 2,000 years ago — far more recently than previous estimates of 9,600 years. The science of the human–cat bond continues to evolve.

2026 — Today 🐾

Cats remain the world's most popular pet alongside dogs, with an estimated 200–600 million living as companions globally. From grain-store pest controllers to internet royalty and beloved family members, the human–cat story is still being written — one purr at a time.

Science Says…

Why Cats Are Good for You

❤️‍🔥

Heart Health

Studies have found that cat owners have a significantly lower risk of heart attack and stroke. The calming effect of a cat's purr can reduce stress hormones and lower blood pressure in minutes.

🧠

Mental Well-being

Interacting with cats releases oxytocin — the bonding hormone. Cat ownership is associated with reduced anxiety, lower depression scores, and an improved sense of purpose and routine.

👶

Childhood Immunity

Children who grow up with cats in their first year of life show a reduced likelihood of developing allergies and asthma later on, as early exposure helps train the immune system.

Did You Know?

Purrfectly Fascinating Facts

🏠

Indoor vs Outdoor

In the US, most pet cats are kept indoors full-time. In the UK, only 26% are always indoors — the rest roam gardens and neighbourhoods freely.

🐭

Master Hunters

Cats retain their hunting instincts entirely. Farm cats and ship's cats have kept pest populations in check for millennia — a purely practical start to a profound friendship.

🧬

Pure vs Mixed

Purebred cats make up less than 10% of the world's cat population. The CFA recognises 41 breeds, 16 of which emerged naturally before selective breeding began.

🤧

Allergies

Up to 3 in 10 Americans are allergic to cats. The culprit is Fel d 1, a protein found in all breed's saliva and dander. Yet many allergic owners happily live with their cats anyway!

👩‍⚕️

Hypoallergenic Dreams

Scientists have worked on engineering cats that produce less Fel d 1. While no truly hypoallergenic cat exists yet, some breeds trigger fewer reactions than others.

🌏

Truly Global

Cats are kept as pets on every permanently inhabited continent. Global estimates range from 200 million to 600 million — making them one of the most widespread companion animals ever.

😴

Sleep Champions

Cats sleep between 12 and 16 hours a day — not laziness, but an evolutionary adaptation for conserving energy between intense bursts of hunting activity.
